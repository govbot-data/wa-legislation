Wilson)READ FIRST TIME 02/09/26.AN ACT Relating to establishing a process for adjudicating tort claims against the state of Washington and its political subdivisions; amending RCW 4.92.030, 4.92.040, 4.96.041, 4.92.110, 4.96.020, 4.92.130, 4.92.160, 4.92.220, 4.92.220, 34.05.425, and 34.12.040; adding a new chapter to Title 4 RCW; creating a new section; providing an effective date; providing an expiration date; and declaring an emergency.BE IT ENACTED BY THE LEGISLATURE OF THE STATE OF WASHINGTON:NEW SECTION.Â Â Sec. 1. The legislature finds that survivors of historical sexual abuse deserve a fair, accessible, and trauma-informed process to seek accountability and resolution. Some claims arising from abuse in government facilities or programs involve events that occurred decades ago. These delays create significant barriers to timely resolution while survivors continue to bear lasting harm. The legislature acknowledges that older claims against the state and its subdivisions present unique challenges, including faded memories, unavailable witnesses, and lost records, which increase costs and prolong litigation for all parties.The legislature therefore finds it necessary to create a claims commission for the purpose of expediting the early resolution of abuse claims arising from government facilities and programs. The purpose of this act is to establish a survivor-centered procedure designed to promote early evaluation, accountability, and resolution of sexual abuse claims, reduce unnecessary litigation, and encourage fair settlement, while fully preserving survivors' constitutional rights and access to the courts. This act is intended to provide a fair, efficient, and trauma-informed process that supports survivors while giving the state and its political subdivisions early opportunities to evaluate claims and pursue resolution.The legislature recognizes that while private entrepreneurs voluntarily choose the ambit of their activity and can thereby exert some control over their exposure to liability, the state and its political subdivisions do not have the same flexibility. In acting for the public good and in responding to public need, state and local governments must provide a broad range of services and perform a broad range of functions throughout the entire state, regardless of how much exposure to liability may be involved. The legislature also recognizes that the state and its political subdivisions provide essential public services and functions, and that unlimited liability could disrupt or make prohibitively expensive the provision of such essential public services and functions.The legislature finds that the right to sue the government is not considered a privilege or immunity requiring heightened scrutiny analysis by the courts. The right to pursue a government tort liability claim in court is not a fundamental right of state citizenship. Article II, section 26 of our state Constitution gives the legislature the sole authority to decide how suits may be brought against the state, so long as the processes conform to the equal protection guarantees of the state and federal Constitutions. A distinction based on the age and type of claims serves a legitimate state interest in that it rationally relates to the purpose of the statute: Addressing old cases and providing all claimants justice by encouraging negotiation and fair settlement, and providing a fair but streamlined resolution that is less expensive and more efficient for all parties involved.The legislature further finds that its power to control and regulate the right of suit against the state and its political subdivisions is plenary. The state may grant the right or refuse it as it chooses. When the state grants the right, it may annex such conditions thereto as it deems wise. Further, the state supreme court has consistently acknowledged that this constitutional duty includes the power to establish conditions precedent to a suit being brought against the state or its political subdivisions, including reasonable procedural burdens that may be placed on tort claimants as long as such burdens are not substantial and do not constitute a real impediment to relief. The legislature therefore finds it necessary to protect the public treasury from increasing liability while providing a structured, fair process for persons who seek compensation for injuries caused by the government.NEW SECTION.Â Â Sec. 2. (1) There is hereby created a claims commission within the office of administrative hearings for the purpose of adjudicating claims against the state and its political subdivisions, or against their officers, employees, or volunteers, acting in such capacity, for damages arising out of tortious conduct.(a) A claim for personal injury arising from alleged tortious conduct in which the claimant's injury occurred 10 or more years before the filing of the claim or when the claimant has reached the age of 18, whichever is later, must be brought in the commission before the claimant may file suit.(b) A claim for personal injury arising from alleged tortious conduct in which the claimant's injury occurred fewer than 10 years before filing or when the claimant has reached the age of 18, whichever is later, may be brought in the commission or as a civil action in a court of competent jurisdiction. If a claimant who previously filed suit brings a claim based on the same tortious conduct in the commission, the court shall dismiss the suit.(c) For purposes of this subsection (1), "tortious conduct" means the specific act or omission alleged to have directly caused the claimant's injury and does not include antecedent policy decisions, design choices, or continuing conditions unrelated to the injury-causing event. For purposes of determining when a continuous course of conduct alleged to have directly caused the claimant's injury occurred, the date of the last act or omission in the continuous course of conduct shall be used.(2) Claims shall be heard and determined using the procedures outlined in chapter 34.05 RCW. The office of administrative hearings shall administer and provide staff support to the claims commission.(3) Members of the claims commission shall be appointed by the governor. The members shall consist of:(a) A representative of the office of the attorney general;(b) A victim advocate;(c) A mental health professional;(d) A representative of the Washington state association for justice;(e) A single representative for all state agencies;(f) A single representative for all counties;(g) A single representative for all school districts;(h) A representative of the office of financial management, who may assign a state actuary; and(i) A representative from the department of enterprise services.(4) The duties of the members of the claims commission include, but are not limited to:(a) Appointing neutral hearing officers consistent with subsection (5) of this section;(b) Maintaining a registry of neutral hearing officers;(c) Adopting rules governing the procedural conduct of hearings, consistent with RCW 34.12.080; and(d) Delivering annual reports on the commission's work to the appropriate committees of the legislature by December 15th of each year. Each report shall include:(i) The number of claims submitted to the commission, including the number accepted for review, pending, resolved, or closed during the reporting period; and(ii) Aggregate information regarding the manner of resolution of claims, including settlement, withdrawal, dismissal, or referral to court, without identifying any claimant or disclosing confidential or privileged information.(5) A neutral hearing officer may set and conduct hearings, determine credibility, and issue written decisions and awards. The neutral hearing officer shall be a retired Washington superior court judge, retired appellate court judge, or former administrative law judge with substantial experience adjudicating tort claims. The neutral hearing officer shall be trained in trauma-informed practices, including recognizing the impacts of trauma on participants and conducting proceedings in a manner that promotes fairness, safety, and respect.NEW SECTION.Â Â Sec. 3. (1) Before filing a claim with the claims commission, a claimant shall comply with the presentment and filing requirements outlined in RCW 4.92.100 and 4.92.110, or RCW 4.96.020, except where a claimant files a claim with the commission after commencing a civil action based on the same tortious conduct. The filing of a claim with the commission shall toll the applicable statute of limitations, but if the claimant commences a civil action and subsequently files a claim with the claims commission based on the same tortious conduct, the court shall dismiss the civil action.(2) Neutral hearing officers shall be assigned on a rotating or random basis, subject to disqualification for cause. Each party shall be entitled to one peremptory strike of an assigned neutral hearing officer, exercised within 10 days of assignment.(3) At the time of filing, a claimant shall select either the expedited examiner track or the hearing track.(4) Expedited examiner track.(a) Claimants electing to proceed on the expedited examiner track shall submit with their claim a statement signed under penalty of perjury identifying the location and approximate dates of abuse, the name of the perpetrator or perpetrators, if known, witnesses, if any, and claimed damages as well as any information in the claimant's possession or control substantiating the claim.(b) Claimants electing the expedited examiner track shall pay a filing fee of $250, except where the claimant is not represented by legal counsel. Should a claimant who was granted a waiver of the filing fee obtain counsel at any time in the proceeding or before payment of an award, the claimant will be assessed a filing fee of $250 to be paid before distribution of the award.(c) Acceptance of an award under the expedited examiner track constitutes a full and final release of all claims arising from the injury.(5) Hearing track.(a) Claimants electing the hearing track shall pay a filing fee of $5,000, except where the claimant is not represented by legal counsel. Should a claimant who was granted a waiver of the filing fee obtain counsel at any time in the proceeding or before payment of an award, the claimant will be assessed a filing fee of $5,000 to be paid before distribution of the award.(b) A neutral hearing officer shall schedule and conduct a preliminary hearing to occur within 60 days of the date the claim is assigned to the hearing track, unless good cause is shown for an extension.(c) Within 60 days of electing the hearing track, the claimant shall provide the neutral hearing officer and the designated government agent a packet of information regarding liability and damages claimed. The packet shall include a statement under penalty of perjury by the claimant regarding the estimated duration and location of the claimed tortious conduct, the name of the perpetrator or perpetrators, the names of any witnesses, claimed damages, and any information in the claimant's possession or control substantiating the claim. The claimant shall also provide relevant records in support of his or her claim.(d) Within 120 days of the date the claimant has served the state or local government agency with the packet, the agency shall provide all records on the claimant related to the alleged tortious conduct, as well as all documents related to other claims arising from the same facility, perpetrator, or placement.(e) At a subsequent hearing, the claimant and a representative of the state or local government agency must appear and be available for examination by the neutral hearing officer. The neutral hearing officer may authorize remote appearances through video conferencing systems.(f) The neutral hearing officer shall issue written findings of fact and conclusions of law within 30 days after the hearing concludes. If the officer finds the state or a political subdivision of the state liable, the officer shall also determine the award.(g) Any aggrieved party may decline to accept the neutral hearing officer's determination. If a party declines the determination, the party may file a case in superior court. Any suit filed must be filed with the clerk of the superior court and served, in accordance with superior court civil rule 5, within 30 days of receipt of the neutral hearing officer's award or decision.(h) The neutral hearing officer's decision, as well as any information exchanged, produced, or disclosed in the hearing process, is confidential and inadmissible in any civil, criminal, or administrative proceeding, except as necessary to enforce a judgment. Nothing in this section precludes a party from obtaining the same underlying information through independent means or through methods otherwise authorized by law or court rule.(i) The superior court shall assess statutory costs and attorneys' fees as set out under RCW 4.84.010 against a claimant who appeals the award and fails to improve his or her position at trial. If the state or a political subdivision declines to accept the decision by the neutral hearing officer and elects to proceed to trial in superior court, and the state or political subdivision fails to improve its position, the superior court shall assess costs and reasonable attorneys' fees against the state or political subdivision that the court finds were reasonably necessary after the state declines the neutral hearing officer's decision.NEW SECTION.Â Â Sec. 4.
